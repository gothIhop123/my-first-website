Moonlight on the Metropolis

A short Zootopia fanfiction — Judy Hopps & Nick Wilde • fanwork

The rain had stopped, but the city still smelled like soft ozone and jasmine. Neon lights reflected off the wet pavement of Tundratown and slid into the warm glow of Sahara Square. Judy Hopps tucked her long ears beneath a hood and pushed her way through a crowd of evening commuters, her badge glinting like a tiny moon in the dim light. She had promised one more stop on her way home — a new lead, a tip that wasn't much more than a whisper, but a whisper she couldn't ignore.

Nick Wilde waited beneath the awning of a corner café, leaning on the counter with his usual, casual grin. He wore a thin scarf tonight and had a half-drunk cup of something smoky steaming at his side. The fox watched Judy with a mixture of amusement and something softer she rarely allowed him to see.

"Late again?" he drawled as she drew near. "You're starting to make this a habit, Officer Hopps." Judy huffed. "Lead's cold, but it's still a lead. Besides— I could ask the same of you. You look suspiciously well-rested for a guy who's supposed to be meeting a smuggler." "Guilty as charged," Nick said, tipping an invisible hat. "But I was thinking more of pastries than contraband."

They walked together through a narrower lane that smelled faintly of cardamom and engine oil. Judy's determination felt less like iron in her ribs and more like a warm thread when Nick fell into step at her side. The case — if it could be called that — had been small: missing deliveries from a food co-op, a pattern in the city that stubbornly refused to become a headline. For Judy it was the sort of thing she lived for; for Nick it was the sort of puzzle that made him want to smile and ask, "What if?"

"The camera near the depot went fuzzy at 23:12," Judy said, eyes scanning a wall where posters curled. "Then a delivery truck that was supposed to be there wasn't. Nothing dramatic, just...odd."

"Someone with a good reason to keep it quiet," Nick offered. "Or someone who's very, very careful." "Either way," Judy said, "we'll find out."

They climbed a narrow staircase that led to a rooftop garden dotted with potted ferns and strings of old bulbs. The city unfurled beneath them like a map stitched in glass and lamplight. From here they could see the depot — a squat building with a neat loading bay — and the shadow of a delivery truck leisurely parked under a streetlamp. Nick raised an eyebrow.

"Want to stake it out?" Judy whispered. "Oh, thrilling," Nick said, but he made no move to leave. He set his cup down and gestured for Judy to sit on an overturned crate next to him. "You take the star role, Officer Hopps. I provide decadent commentary."

They waited.

At first, nothing happened — only the soft murmur of traffic and a stray cat slipping shadows. Then, as the city clock chimed midnight, a figure moved at the depot's side: a large silhouette, careful and sure. Two others followed. Watching through a pair of borrowed binoculars, Judy's nose twitched the way it did when a clue clicked into place.

"They're unloading something small," she whispered. "Crates. Maybe foodstuffs?" Nick leaned in, squinting. "Maybe they're trying to do it without cameras noticing."

Judy's mind started to map the scene — angles, exits, the way the three moved in rhythm. She could feel the old adrenaline, the same thrill that had made her take on the city in the first place. She reached for her radio and then paused. For a moment she allowed the night to exist without orders, without sirens. Nick watched her, and for the first time in a long stretch of cases and coffee-fueled nights, she felt like she wasn't alone in carrying the weight of a missing clue.

They moved in, not with sirens but with quiet steps and shared purpose. The trio of shadowy figures hadn't expected an audience. Judy slipped from the alley like a whisper, Nick trailing, his tail low and careful. The crates were humble — boxes labeled with the co-op logo. When one crate opened the scent of dried herbs swooped out, innocent and domestic. The middle crate, however, contained a cache of small devices — trackers and modules, not the sort of thing you'd expect in a food delivery.

"Why hide trackers in food crates?" Nick muttered. "That's... oddly clever." "They're using the deliveries to map routes — not to steal the goods, but to map stops," Judy said. "Someone's building a picture of the network."

As they moved closer, the three men turned, startled. Judy held up her badge slowly. "ZPD," she said, voice steady. "We need to ask some questions."

The confrontation was brisk. It turned out the men were contractors hired by a logistics company working for a larger pack-runner — a broker who'd been offering route optimization to the bigger delivery firms. Their intent had never been malicious: the trackers were there to test efficiency. But in the wrong hands, Judy knew, a map could become a blueprint.

They untangled the misunderstanding with paperwork and statements, and the men left with their dignity slightly bruised but cooperation intact. The broker, when traced, was a different story. He owed money to a syndicate that loved routes and hated witnesses. Nick raised a brow and pocketed a small, folded business card the men had left behind — an address, a safehouse maybe, or the next piece of the puzzle. He passed it to Judy with the smile that was equal parts apology and invitation.

"You planning to arrest him now, or later?" he asked. Judy set her jaw. "Now, Nick."

Their trip to the broker's office took them across neighborhoods they knew well — the places where the city hummed in different registers. By the time they reached the address, night had softened into pre-dawn. The broker's office smelled of cheap coffee and old paper. He was surprised, flustered, and ultimately led quietly into cuffs. It wasn't glamorous, but it was right.

On the walk home, the sky blushed with the first light. Judy felt it: the small, clean satisfaction of having done good work. Nick nudged her shoulder gently. "You ever think about how small moments like this add up?" he asked. She considered it. "All the time. But it's better when you don't have to carry them alone."

Nick's grin softened into something real. "Good. Because I don't plan on letting you."
